## Executive Summary

The learning management system market enters 2026 at an inflection point. Valued at **$26.8 billion in 2025**, the global LMS market is projected to reach **$100.6 billion by 2034**, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of **15.84%** (IMARC Group, 2026). But raw growth figures tell only part of the story. The market is being reshaped by forces that are fundamentally changing what an LMS is, who buys one, and why.

Three structural shifts define this moment:

**AI has moved from feature to foundation.** Every major LMS vendor now ships AI capabilities — from adaptive learning paths and automated content generation to AI tutoring and predictive analytics. The question is no longer whether a platform has AI, but how deeply it's integrated and how effectively it serves learners.

**Consolidation is accelerating.** The landmark **Coursera–Udemy merger** ($2.5 billion, announced December 2025) sent a clear signal: scale matters, and the era of fragmented point solutions is ending. Docebo acquired 365Talents for skills intelligence. Instructure absorbed Parchment for credentialing. The LMS market is entering its roll-up phase.

**The buyer base is diversifying.** Corporate training, customer education, and expert-led learning businesses are growing faster than traditional academic use cases. Companies like Datadog, GoDaddy, and ActiveCampaign now run learning programs on platforms originally built for independent experts. The lines between corporate LMS, course platform, and community tool are blurring.

This report examines the LMS market across three buyer personas that represent the majority of purchasing decisions in 2026:

- **Experts** — Independent professionals, consultants, coaches, and thought leaders monetizing their knowledge through courses, communities, and memberships.
- **Academies** — Training organizations, certification bodies, bootcamps, and educational institutions delivering structured learning programs at scale.
- **Companies** — Businesses using learning platforms for customer education, partner training, employee onboarding, and compliance.

For each, the calculus is shifting. The platforms that win in 2026 will be those that combine AI-native capabilities, community tools, commerce infrastructure, and credentialing — not just content delivery.

These figures encompass the full spectrum of LMS solutions — academic, corporate, and independent. The corporate segment is growing fastest, driven by cloud-based deployment and AI-powered personalization.

### Regional Distribution

**North America** dominates with **43.2%** of the global market share in 2025, with the United States accounting for approximately **75.9%** of the North American market. This dominance reflects the region's mature corporate training infrastructure, high EdTech adoption rates, and the concentration of major LMS vendors.

**Asia-Pacific** is the fastest-growing region, fueled by digital transformation in manufacturing, SME proliferation (India's MSME base is projected to grow from 6.38 crore to 7.5 crore at 2.5% CAGR), and government investment in workforce development.

**Europe** is growing steadily, with the European Commission targeting 75% of businesses using cloud-edge technologies by 2030 — a policy framework that directly supports LMS adoption. Multilingual requirements and GDPR compliance shape the region's platform preferences.

### Segment Breakdown

By user type, **academic institutions** still hold the largest share at **60.2%** of the market (IMARC, 2025), but **corporate LMS** is growing at nearly **19.1% CAGR** (Custom Market Insights), significantly outpacing the academic segment. This divergence reflects the explosive growth in workforce upskilling, compliance training, and customer education programs.

By delivery mode, **distance learning** leads at **52.1%**, followed by blended learning and instructor-led training. The pandemic permanently shifted baseline expectations: hybrid and online formats are now default, not alternative.

The global e-learning market has grown more than **900% since 2000** and is projected to surpass **$370 billion in 2026** (IMARC). Within this broader context, LMS platforms serve as the critical infrastructure layer — the operating system for digital learning.

### Adjacent Markets

Several adjacent markets are expanding in parallel, reinforcing LMS growth:

## Key Trends Shaping 2026

### 1. AI: From Feature to Foundation

AI is the defining trend of the LMS market in 2026. But the conversation has shifted from "does your platform have AI?" to "how mature is your AI implementation?"

**What's shipping now:**

- **AI content generation**: Platforms like Thinkific (Thinker AI Agents, launched February 2026), Kajabi (AI Creator Hub, reducing content creation time by up to 90%), and Coursera (partnership with OpenAI and Anthropic) have shipped AI tools that help experts and organizations generate course content, quizzes, and assessments in minutes rather than days.

- **Adaptive learning paths**: AI systems analyze learner behavior, performance, and preferences to dynamically adjust content sequence, pacing, and difficulty. This moves the LMS from a static content delivery system to a responsive learning environment.

- **AI tutoring and support**: Coursera's Coach AI tutor, Udemy's AI-powered microlearning, and Thinkific's Thinker represent a new category of AI teaching assistants that provide 24/7 learner support, answer questions in context, and guide students through difficult concepts.

- **Predictive analytics**: Machine learning models now identify at-risk learners before they drop out, enabling proactive intervention. This is particularly valuable for academies and companies running cohort-based programs where completion rates directly impact revenue and outcomes.

- **Automated assessment and feedback**: AI-powered grading of open-ended assignments, code submissions, and written work is moving from experimental to production. This dramatically reduces the operational burden for experts running courses at scale.

**What's emerging:**

- **AI agents**: The concept of autonomous AI agents that handle enrollment, student support, progress monitoring, and even personalized coaching is gaining traction. Thinkific's Thinker AI Agents allow customers to create custom agents trained on their own proprietary data — a significant step toward AI-powered learning experiences that feel bespoke.

- **Skills intelligence layers**: Docebo's January 2026 acquisition of 365Talents signals a shift toward AI that doesn't just deliver content but maps skills, identifies gaps, and connects learning to workforce readiness. This "intelligence layer" connecting skills data to learning paths represents the next frontier in corporate LMS.

- **AI-native platform design**: Rather than bolting AI onto existing architectures, a new generation of platforms (like Disco and others) are building AI into the core experience from the ground up. These platforms treat AI not as a feature module but as the foundational technology layer.

**The risk:** As Coursera acknowledged in recent SEC filings, AI could "displace or otherwise adversely impact the demand for online learning solutions." The cautionary tale is Chegg, which laid off nearly half its workforce after ChatGPT and Google's AI Overviews eroded demand for its homework help services. LMS platforms must ensure their AI capabilities create moats, not commoditize their offerings.

### 2. Customer Education Goes Mainstream

The fastest-growing use case for LMS platforms in 2026 isn't traditional e-learning — it's **customer education**. SaaS companies, service providers, and product-led businesses are discovering that teaching customers drives retention, reduces support costs, and accelerates expansion revenue.

Companies like **Datadog, GoDaddy, ActiveCampaign, and Nasdaq** now run customer education programs on platforms like Thinkific Plus, generating measurable business impact. This isn't a niche use case — it's becoming a standard part of the SaaS playbook.

The customer education platform market is now tracked independently by research firms, with APO Research publishing a dedicated Global Customer Education and Training Software Market Outlook in early 2026. This recognition as a distinct market segment validates what practitioners have known for years: customer education is a business function, not a side project.

**Key drivers:**

- **Retention economics**: It costs 5–7x more to acquire a new customer than retain an existing one. Educated customers churn less, expand more, and advocate louder.
- **Support cost reduction**: Self-service learning deflects support tickets. Companies report 30–50% reductions in support volume after launching education programs.
- **Product-led growth**: Customer academies serve as discovery engines, introducing users to features and use cases they haven't explored.
- **B2B selling**: Thinkific reported 17% growth in its Plus (enterprise) segment in Q4 2025, with B2B selling features like bulk licenses, invoicing, and subscription management driving adoption.

For **Companies** evaluating LMS platforms, customer education capability is now a primary selection criterion — not an afterthought.

### 3. Consolidation Reshapes the Competitive Landscape

The LMS market entered a new consolidation cycle in 2025–2026, with several landmark transactions reshaping the competitive landscape:

**Coursera acquires Udemy ($2.5 billion, December 2025):** The most significant EdTech merger in a decade. The all-stock deal combines Coursera's 191 million registered learners and 375+ university partnerships with Udemy's 82 million learners and 220,000+ course marketplace. The merged entity will serve 270+ million learners, generate $1.5+ billion in annual revenue, and target $115 million in annual cost synergies. The deal is expected to close in H2 2026.

**Docebo acquires 365Talents (January 2026):** The Toronto-based enterprise learning platform acquired Lyon-based 365Talents to build an AI-powered skills intelligence layer connecting learning to workforce readiness. This positions Docebo at the intersection of LMS and skills management — a strategic bet on the convergence of these categories.

**Instructure acquires Parchment (completed 2024):** The Canvas parent company absorbed the world's largest academic credentialing platform, adding lifelong credential management to its teaching and learning ecosystem. This move signals that LMS vendors are expanding their definition of "learning" to include credentialing, verification, and career portability.

**2U/edX restructuring:** The 2021 acquisition of edX by 2U for $800 million has been followed by significant financial pressure, with 2U facing profitability challenges. The for-profit ownership of a mission-driven platform continues to create tension in the market.

**Vista Equity Partners / Pluralsight:** The $3.5 billion take-private deal in 2021 provided Pluralsight with resources for expansion, but private equity ownership typically brings margin pressure and efficiency mandates that reshape product investment priorities.

**What's driving consolidation:**

- **Unit economics**: Customer acquisition costs have risen dramatically in digital marketing. Scale improves marketing efficiency and amortizes content costs across larger audiences.
- **AI investment requirements**: Building competitive AI capabilities requires substantial engineering talent and compute resources. Smaller platforms cannot match the AI investment of well-funded competitors.
- **Enterprise buyer preferences**: Corporate buyers prefer consolidated vendor relationships. A single platform serving multiple learning needs wins over a portfolio of point solutions.
- **Content cost inflation**: Competition for quality instructors and content has driven up production costs. Larger platforms can amortize these investments more effectively.

**Implications for buyers:** The consolidation wave means fewer independent alternatives, but also more capable platforms with broader feature sets. Buyers should evaluate vendor financial health and strategic independence alongside feature comparisons.

### 4. Mobile Learning Reaches Maturity

Mobile learning has evolved from a nice-to-have responsive design feature to a primary consumption channel, particularly for expert-led content and corporate training.

**Key statistics:**
- **40% of Kajabi content** is now consumed via mobile devices, prompting the platform to invest in branded mobile apps with push notifications, community tools, and live streaming.
- Mobile learning is projected to grow significantly through 2031, with dedicated market reports from Mordor Intelligence and other firms tracking the segment.
- **63% of K-12 students** in the US participate daily in online learning, with mobile as a primary access device.

**What's changed in 2026:**
- **Branded mobile apps**: Platforms like Kajabi and Thinkific now offer white-label mobile applications, giving experts and academies their own branded presence in app stores. This shifts the relationship from "your students use our platform" to "your students use your app."
- **Offline-first capabilities**: As LMS platforms expand into emerging markets where connectivity is inconsistent, offline content access has become a competitive differentiator.
- **Micro-learning optimization**: Mobile consumption patterns favor shorter, focused content modules. Platforms are redesigning content architectures around 5–15 minute learning segments optimized for mobile screens.
- **Push notification engagement**: Mobile apps enable direct learner engagement through notifications — a channel that dramatically outperforms email for re-engagement and completion nudges.

### 5. Micro-Credentials and Digital Badges Gain Institutional Acceptance

The digital badges market reached **$0.96 billion in 2025** and is projected to grow substantially through 2032. But the real story isn't market size — it's institutional acceptance.

**Key developments:**

- **Instructure's acquisition of Parchment** creates an integrated pathway from learning (Canvas LMS) to credentialing (Parchment verification), suggesting that major LMS vendors see credential management as a core capability, not an add-on.
- **Employers increasingly recognize micro-credentials**: The World Economic Forum's estimate that half of all employees need reskilling by 2025 has accelerated employer acceptance of non-degree credentials as signals of capability.
- **Stackable credentials**: The most effective credential systems allow learners to accumulate micro-credentials that stack into larger certifications or are recognized for academic credit. This "building block" approach serves all three buyer personas — experts can issue badges, academies can build credential pathways, and companies can track employee skill acquisition.
- **Blockchain and verifiable credentials**: While still early, the move toward tamper-proof, portable digital credentials is gaining momentum. Standards like Open Badges 3.0 and Verifiable Credentials are maturing.

**For experts**, micro-credentials add tangible value to course completion. A certificate from a recognized expert, verified and portable, is worth more than a PDF completion notice.

**For academies**, stackable credential systems increase student lifetime value by creating clear progression paths from introductory courses to advanced certifications.

**For companies**, skill-based credentialing enables more precise workforce planning and creates internal mobility pathways that improve retention.

### 6. Community Becomes a Core LMS Feature

The integration of community features into LMS platforms is no longer experimental — it's table stakes. The data supports it: Kajabi's community offers grew from 23,000 in 2021 to 38,000 in 2023, a 65% increase. Thinkific invested substantially in community features throughout 2025, including modernized UI, weekly digest emails, rich text and multi-image posts.

**Why community matters:**

- **Retention**: Learners who participate in communities complete courses at significantly higher rates than those who learn in isolation. The social accountability and peer support mechanisms of cohort-based and community-driven learning address the completion rate problem that has plagued self-paced online courses.
- **Revenue**: Communities create recurring revenue opportunities through memberships and subscriptions, complementing one-time course purchases with ongoing engagement models.
- **Network effects**: Active communities attract new learners through word-of-mouth and social proof, reducing customer acquisition costs.
- **Differentiation**: In a market where course content is increasingly commoditized (especially with AI-generated content), community and human connection become the differentiator that cannot be replicated.

**Cohort-based learning** is gaining momentum as a model that combines the scalability of online platforms with the engagement and accountability of synchronous group experiences. Platforms like Maven, Disco, and dedicated cohort features in existing LMS platforms are serving this demand.

For all three buyer personas, community transforms a transactional product (buy a course) into an ongoing relationship (join a learning community). This shift has profound implications for business models, engagement metrics, and platform selection criteria.

### 7. Video Evolution: From Lectures to Interactive Experiences

Video remains the dominant content format in online learning, but the definition of "video content" is evolving rapidly in 2026. The video learning platform market is expected to reach **$20 billion by 2035** at a 9.1% CAGR.

**Key shifts:**

- **Short-form educational video**: Influenced by TikTok and YouTube Shorts consumption patterns, learners increasingly expect concise, focused video content. Platforms are adapting by supporting micro-lesson formats alongside traditional long-form lectures.
- **Interactive video**: Features like in-video quizzes, branching scenarios, and clickable elements transform passive watching into active learning. AI is enabling automatic generation of interactive elements within existing video content.
- **AI-generated video**: Tools for creating AI-narrated, AI-animated educational videos are reducing production costs and enabling rapid content iteration. While quality varies, the trajectory is clear: video production is becoming faster and cheaper.
- **Live streaming integration**: The integration of live video (webinars, workshops, Q&A sessions) directly within LMS platforms blurs the line between synchronous and asynchronous learning, enabling hybrid models that combine the engagement of live interaction with the scalability of recorded content.
- **Video analytics**: AI-powered analysis of video engagement (where learners pause, rewatch, skip, or drop off) provides insights that enable content optimization at a granular level.

## Buyer Behavior

Purchase decisions for LMS platforms are increasingly driven by specific use cases rather than generic feature comparisons. Here's how each buyer persona approaches the market in 2026:

### Experts

**Profile:** Independent professionals, consultants, coaches, authors, and thought leaders selling courses, memberships, and digital products.

**What they prioritize:**
1. **Ease of use** — Experts need to launch quickly without technical teams. Drag-and-drop builders, templates, and AI-assisted content creation are table stakes.
2. **Commerce capabilities** — Payment processing, checkout optimization, coupon management, and revenue analytics. Thinkific's commerce revenue grew 32% in 2025, reflecting this priority.
3. **Brand control** — Custom domains, branded mobile apps, and white-label experiences. Experts want students to see *their* brand, not the platform's.
4. **Community tools** — Building an audience community around their expertise for ongoing engagement and recurring revenue.
5. **Pricing transparency** — Experts typically operate as solo businesses or small teams; predictable costs matter.

**Platforms gaining traction:** Kajabi (all-in-one), Thinkific (learning commerce), Podia (simplicity), Teachable (creator-focused).

**Emerging behavior:** Experts increasingly launch with a community or membership first, then add courses. The "course-first" model is giving way to "audience-first" approaches where community engagement precedes and informs content creation.

**Revenue context:** Kajabi reports that its experts have generated over **$9 billion in cumulative revenue** on the platform. The average Kajabi expert earns 25% more than those selling exclusively through social media channels. 89% of Kajabi experts have fewer than 50,000 Instagram followers — evidence that platform-enabled expertise monetization doesn't require massive audiences.

### Academies

**Profile:** Training organizations, certification bodies, bootcamps, professional development firms, and educational institutions.

**What they prioritize:**
1. **Scalable program management** — Multiple courses, instructors, cohorts, and credential paths managed from a single platform.
2. **Credentialing and certification** — Issuing recognized credentials, managing continuing education credits, and maintaining compliance with accreditation standards.
3. **Analytics and reporting** — Detailed learner progress tracking, completion rates, assessment performance, and program-level metrics. Academies need data for both operational management and accreditation reporting.
4. **Cohort management** — The ability to run time-bound programs with defined start dates, group activities, and synchronous components.
5. **Integration** — LMS must connect with CRM, payment processing, marketing automation, and student information systems.

**Platforms gaining traction:** Thinkific Plus, D2L Brightspace, Docebo, Canvas.

**Emerging behavior:** Academies are increasingly building "learning stacks" — combining an LMS with complementary tools for community (Circle, Slack), video (Zoom, Loom), and credentialing (Credly, Parchment). The most sophisticated academies are consolidating these stacks onto platforms that offer integrated alternatives.

### Companies

**Profile:** SaaS businesses, service companies, and enterprises using LMS for customer education, partner training, employee onboarding, and compliance.

**What they prioritize:**
1. **Customer-facing experience** — For customer education, the LMS is a product touchpoint. Design quality, UX, and brand consistency matter as much as content delivery.
2. **Integration with business systems** — SSO, CRM integration (Salesforce, HubSpot), analytics integration, and API access for custom workflows.
3. **Measurable business outcomes** — Companies need to tie learning activity to business metrics: reduced support tickets, higher product adoption, improved NPS, lower churn.
4. **Scalability and reliability** — Enterprise SLAs, uptime guarantees, and the ability to handle thousands of concurrent learners without performance degradation.
5. **B2B commerce capabilities** — Bulk licensing, invoicing, seat management, and subscription billing for selling training to business customers.

**Platforms gaining traction:** Thinkific Plus, Docebo, Absorb, Skilljar, WorkRamp.

**Emerging behavior:** Companies are moving customer education from "marketing initiative" to "business function" with dedicated teams, budgets, and KPIs. The most mature organizations have a VP or Director of Customer Education reporting to the Chief Customer Officer or Chief Revenue Officer.

### Cross-Persona Trends

Several behavioral patterns span all three personas:

- **Free trials and self-serve onboarding** are expected. Platforms requiring sales calls for evaluation are losing deals to those offering immediate trial access.
- **Community reputation matters more than vendor marketing.** Buyers consult peer communities, review sites, and social proof before engaging vendors.
- **Total cost of ownership (TCO)** increasingly includes implementation time, integration costs, and opportunity cost of platform limitations — not just subscription pricing.
- **AI capabilities are becoming a selection criterion.** Buyers are actively evaluating AI features during platform selection, not treating them as future nice-to-haves.

## Pricing Trends

LMS pricing in 2026 reflects the market's maturation and the increasing sophistication of buyer needs. Several notable trends are reshaping how platforms monetize:

### The Shift to Value-Based Pricing

Platforms are moving from seat-based or flat-rate pricing toward models that align costs with the value delivered:

- **Revenue-based commerce fees**: Thinkific's commerce revenue model (processing fees on transactions through Thinkific Payments) grew 32% in 2025. This model aligns platform revenue with customer success — the platform earns more when its customers sell more.
- **Usage-based AI pricing**: As AI features become central to platform value, some vendors are introducing tiered AI access or usage-based pricing for AI capabilities like content generation, tutoring hours, and advanced analytics.
- **Outcome-based enterprise contracts**: For large company deployments, pricing discussions increasingly reference expected outcomes (support ticket deflection, onboarding time reduction) rather than pure seat counts.

### Platform Pricing Snapshot (March 2026)

**Expert-focused platforms** typically range from **$39–$399/month** for standard plans, with enterprise or advanced tiers reaching $499+/month:

- **Thinkific**: Free tier available; paid plans from Basic through Plus (enterprise); ARPU of $175/month in Q4 2025.
- **Kajabi**: Plans range from Basic ($149/month) to Pro ($399/month); additional revenue from Kajabi Payments processing fees; conversion rate improvements of up to 10% with Apple Pay, Google Pay, and Afterpay integrations.
- **Teachable**: Tiered pricing with transaction fees on lower plans, reduced or eliminated fees on higher tiers.
- **Podia**: Simplified pricing with fewer tiers; positioned as the budget-friendly option.

**Enterprise LMS platforms** typically price per-user with annual contracts:

- Docebo, Absorb, Cornerstone, and similar platforms negotiate custom pricing based on user count, features required, and contract length. Enterprise deals typically range from $15,000–$200,000+ annually.

**Marketplace/MOOC platforms** use hybrid models:

- **Coursera Plus**: ~$399/year for individual learners; enterprise pricing based on seat count.
- **Udemy**: Pay-per-course ($10–$200 per course) with frequent promotional pricing; Udemy Business priced per-seat for enterprise.
- Post-merger, the combined Coursera–Udemy entity will likely move toward unified subscription models with tiered access levels.

### Key Pricing Trends

1. **Free tiers are expanding.** Platforms like Thinkific maintain generous free tiers as acquisition channels, betting that successful experts will graduate to paid plans as they scale.

2. **Transaction fees are replacing seat fees** for expert and academy use cases. This reduces upfront cost barriers and aligns platform economics with customer growth.

3. **AI premium pricing is emerging.** Advanced AI features (custom AI agents, AI-generated content, predictive analytics) are being packaged into higher-tier plans or offered as add-ons.

4. **Annual contracts offer meaningful discounts.** Monthly-to-annual pricing gaps of 20–40% encourage longer commitments, improving vendor revenue predictability.

5. **B2B pricing complexity is increasing.** As platforms serve companies for customer education, pricing now includes considerations for multiple sub-accounts, reseller models, white-label licensing, and API access tiers.

## Predictions for 2027

Based on current trajectory analysis, market signals, and industry interviews, we project the following developments for the LMS market in 2027:

### 1. AI Agents Become Standard LMS Infrastructure

The AI tutoring and support capabilities shipping in 2026 (Thinkific's Thinker, Coursera's Coach, etc.) will evolve into fully autonomous AI agents that handle enrollment support, learner onboarding, progress monitoring, content recommendations, and first-line troubleshooting. By late 2027, leading platforms will offer AI agents that can be trained on proprietary content and deployed without technical expertise. This will fundamentally change the economics of running a learning business — a solo expert will be able to provide 24/7 personalized learner support that previously required a team.

### 2. The Coursera–Udemy Merger Triggers a Second Wave of Consolidation

The merger, expected to close in H2 2026, will pressure competitors to pursue their own scale-building strategies. We expect at least 2–3 significant M&A transactions in the LMS/EdTech space in 2027, likely involving:
- LinkedIn Learning making an acquisition to compete with the combined Coursera–Udemy platform.
- Private equity roll-ups of mid-market LMS vendors into broader learning technology platforms.
- An enterprise LMS vendor acquiring a community or creator-focused platform to serve the expert and academy segments.

### 3. Customer Education Becomes a Recognized Business Function

By 2027, "Customer Education" will be a standard departmental function at SaaS companies with 500+ employees, similar to how "Customer Success" evolved from an emerging concept to a standard function over the past decade. This will drive dedicated budgets, headcount, and platform procurement cycles specifically for customer education LMS solutions. The customer education platform market will grow at 25%+ annually, outpacing the broader LMS market.

### 4. Community-First Models Overtake Course-First Models

The traditional "build a course, sell a course" model will be surpassed by community-first approaches where experts and academies build engaged audiences first, then monetize through courses, memberships, coaching, and events within the community. Platforms that treat community as an add-on will lose ground to those that treat it as the foundational engagement layer.

### 5. Micro-Credential Interoperability Emerges

Standards for micro-credential portability and recognition will mature, with major employers and platforms adopting common frameworks (building on Open Badges 3.0 and related standards). This interoperability will make credentials issued on one platform recognizable and verifiable across others, increasing the value of credential-issuing capabilities for all LMS platforms.

### 6. Mobile Becomes the Primary Learning Channel for Experts' Audiences

For expert-led learning businesses, mobile will surpass desktop as the primary content consumption channel by late 2027. This will force platform investment in native mobile experiences, offline capabilities, and mobile-optimized content creation tools. Experts and academies without a mobile strategy will see declining engagement and completion rates.

### 7. AI-Generated Content Abundance Creates Quality Verification Demand

As AI makes content creation trivially easy, the market will face a content abundance problem. The differentiation will shift from content quantity to content quality and verification. Platforms will invest in quality scoring, expert curation, peer review mechanisms, and credential-based content authority signals to help learners navigate the abundance.

### 8. The LMS Category Label Evolves

The term "Learning Management System" will increasingly feel inadequate for platforms that combine learning, community, commerce, credentialing, and AI tutoring. Expect the emergence of new category labels — "learning commerce platform," "learning experience ecosystem," or similar — as vendors and analysts attempt to describe the evolved capabilities. Thinkific's self-description as a "learning commerce platform" is an early indicator of this category evolution.

### 9. Regional LMS Leaders Emerge in Asia and Latin America

While North American platforms dominate globally, regional LMS leaders will emerge to serve local markets with better localization, local payment methods, regional compliance, and culturally appropriate content and community features. The Coursera–Udemy merger's geographic complementarity acknowledges this dynamic — international markets represent the highest-growth opportunity.

### 10. Pricing Models Converge Toward Hybrid Revenue Share

The market will move toward a consensus pricing model combining a low base subscription fee with revenue-sharing or transaction-based fees. This model, already demonstrated by Thinkific's commerce revenue growth (32% YoY in 2025), reduces barriers to entry while aligning platform economics with customer success. Platforms charging high flat fees with no success-based component will face competitive pressure.

### Definitions

**Learning Management System (LMS)**: For this report, we use a broad definition encompassing any software platform used to create, deliver, manage, and track online learning experiences. This includes traditional enterprise LMS, course creation platforms, learning experience platforms (LXP), MOOC platforms, and learning commerce platforms.

**Three Buyer Personas**:
- **Experts**: Independent professionals monetizing their knowledge through digital products. Includes coaches, consultants, authors, speakers, and subject-matter authorities.
- **Academies**: Organizations whose primary business is education and training delivery. Includes bootcamps, certification bodies, professional development firms, and traditional educational institutions.
- **Companies**: Businesses using learning platforms for internal training, customer education, partner enablement, or compliance. The learning platform supports a broader business objective rather than being the primary product.
