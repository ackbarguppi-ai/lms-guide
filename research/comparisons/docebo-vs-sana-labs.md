# Docebo vs Sana Labs: Enterprise LMS Heavyweights Compared (2026)

**The short answer:** Docebo is the safer pick for complex, multi-audience enterprise training with deep compliance and partner enablement needs. Sana Labs is the sharper pick if your priority is AI-native learning, fast deployment, and you're willing to bet on the newer platform — especially if you're already in the Workday ecosystem.

Neither is cheap. Both are serious enterprise tools. The right choice depends on what you're actually building your training program to do.

## Platform Origins and Philosophy

These two platforms come from fundamentally different eras of learning technology, and it shows in everything from their architecture to their sales pitch.

### Docebo: The Evolved Enterprise Veteran

Docebo has been in the LMS game since 2005. Over two decades, it has grown from a straightforward course-management tool into a full enterprise learning suite with AI capabilities, e-commerce, gamification, compliance automation, and white-labeling. It went public on the Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX: DCBO) and now serves over 3,800 companies globally.

Its AI layer — branded "Harmony" — handles content recommendations, search, skills tracking, and a virtual coaching feature. But the AI sits on top of a mature, feature-rich platform, not the other way around. Docebo's strength is breadth and configurability: it can serve employees, partners, customers, and channel resellers from a single instance with deeply segmented experiences.

### Sana Labs: The AI-First Challenger

Sana Labs was founded in 2016 in Stockholm with AI as the foundational architecture, not a bolt-on. Rather than building a traditional LMS and adding intelligence later, Sana built adaptive learning, knowledge retrieval, and AI-powered authoring into the platform's core from day one.

The big headline: Workday acquired Sana Labs for approximately $1.1 billion in late 2025 — the largest acquisition in Workday's history. Sana Learn continues operating as a standalone product, but the Workday integration opens up powerful talent-data synergies for organizations already in that ecosystem.

Sana positions itself as four tools in one: an LMS, a learning experience platform (LXP), a content authoring tool, and a virtual classroom. Its pitch is that consolidating these functions with an AI layer connecting them is worth more than running four separate best-of-breed tools.

## Pricing Breakdown

Neither platform publishes transparent, self-serve pricing. Both require a sales conversation for enterprise deals. Here's what we know from verified quotes, published tiers, and market analysis.

### Docebo Pricing

Docebo uses two tiers: **Elevate** (mid-market entry point) and **Enterprise** (full-featured).

| Tier | Estimated Annual Cost | Per-User/Month (est.) | Key Inclusions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Elevate | $30,000–$45,000 (500 users) | $5–$7.50 | Core LMS, up to 4 integrations, AI features (credit-based), standard support |
| Enterprise | $70,000+ (1,000 users verified) | $5.83–$10+ | Everything in Elevate + Advanced Analytics, Branded Mobile App, up to 6 integrations, premium support |

**Important cost factors:**
- **AI credit system** (launched January 2026): Premium AI features like Virtual Coaching (55 credits/voice session) and AI Video Presenter (210 credits/minute) run on consumption-based credits. Overage is auto-billed — no hard cutoff.
- **Add-ons** priced separately: E-Commerce, Gamification, Immersive Learning, Salesforce/Teams integrations
- **Implementation**: Essential onboarding included (8-week timeline, up to 100K records migration). Complex deployments cost extra.
- **First-year TCO**: Plan for 50–150% above base subscription when factoring in implementation, content development, integrations, and AI credits.

### Sana Labs Pricing

Sana uses a simpler structure, though enterprise pricing still requires a sales conversation.

| Tier | Price | Minimum | Key Inclusions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free | $0 | None | Limited features, individual/small team use |
| Team | $30/user/month | N/A | Core AI features, integrations, up to ~50 users |
| Core (Sana Learn) | $8–$13/user/month | 300 users | AI authoring, adaptive learning, live classroom, analytics |
| Enterprise (Sana Learn) | Custom quote | Custom | Core + SSO, SCIM, open API, HRIS connectors, SLA-backed support |

**Important cost factors:**
- **300-user minimum** on Core: A company with 150 employees effectively pays double per user since they must cover 300 seats (~$46,800/year minimum at $13/user/month).
- **Implementation**: 2–4 weeks typical, significantly faster than legacy platforms. AI-powered SCORM/xAPI importer handles migration.
- **Enterprise add-ons**: HRIS integrations, identity providers, custom SLAs, and priority support all influence final pricing.
- **Renewals**: Watch for price adjustments at renewal unless negotiated upfront.

### Pricing Verdict

At comparable scale (500–1,000 users), both platforms land in similar territory: roughly $50,000–$80,000+ annually. Docebo's volume discounts at 1,000+ users are aggressive ($5.83/user/month verified), while Sana's lower published starting price looks attractive but gets complicated by the 300-seat minimum and enterprise add-ons.

Docebo has more hidden cost surface area (AI credits, add-on modules, tier-gated features). Sana has a simpler pricing model but less pricing transparency at the enterprise level.

## Category Breakdown

### 1. AI Capabilities

**Winner: Sana Labs**

This is Sana's core differentiator. The platform was built AI-first, and it shows:

- **AI authoring**: Feed it a PDF, manual, or document and it generates structured courses with assessments. Polestar reported saving ~900 hours by converting existing materials into interactive modules.
- **AI tutor**: Uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounded in your organization's actual data — Slack messages, SharePoint docs, GitHub repos, Salesforce records. Not generic chatbot answers.
- **Semantic search**: Indexes across all connected apps with sub-100ms latency. Understands meaning, not just keywords.
- **Natural-language analytics**: Type a question like "which managers in EMEA have the lowest completion rates?" and get a visualization.

Docebo's AI is capable but architecturally different. Its "Harmony" system handles content recommendations, search, and skills tracking. The newer AI features — Virtual Coaching, AI Video Presenter, AI-assisted course creation — are powerful but operate on a consumption-based credit system that adds cost complexity. Docebo's AI feels like a sophisticated addition to the platform; Sana's AI feels like *the platform itself*.

### 2. Content Creation and Authoring

**Winner: Sana Labs**

Sana's authoring environment is frequently described as "Figma-like" — collaborative, drag-and-drop, designed so subject matter experts (not just instructional designers) can build solid content without specialist training. Built-in DeepL translation covers 50+ languages natively.

G2 scores back this up: Sana scores 9.1 for Content Creation vs. Docebo's 7.1. That's a massive gap.

Docebo offers course creation tools and AI-assisted lesson generation (10 credits per lesson), but content creation has traditionally been an area where Docebo users rely on third-party authoring tools like Articulate 360 or Captivate.

### 3. Multi-Audience and Extended Enterprise

**Winner: Docebo**

This is where Docebo's two decades of enterprise experience shine. If you need to train employees, onboard customers, certify partners, and enable resellers — all from a single platform with segmented, white-labeled experiences — Docebo is purpose-built for this.

Docebo's extended enterprise capabilities include:
- Multi-tenant architecture with audience-specific branding
- Customer education portals with e-commerce
- Partner certification programs
- Channel enablement with tiered access

Sana Labs is primarily focused on internal employee training. While it can support some external use cases, it lacks the depth of Docebo's multi-audience infrastructure, e-commerce engine, and partner/customer training specialization.

### 4. Compliance and Certifications

**Winner: Docebo**

Docebo has deep compliance automation: certificate generation, automatic re-enrollment for expiring certifications, industry-specific compliance tracking, and audit trails built from years of serving regulated industries (healthcare, finance, manufacturing).

Sana Labs doesn't currently offer certification engines or the kind of compliance infrastructure that heavily regulated organizations require. G2 data confirms: Docebo has established Healthcare LMS ratings; Sana doesn't have enough data in this category.

### 5. Integrations

**Winner: Docebo**

Docebo offers 400+ integrations out of the box, including deep connections with Salesforce, Microsoft Teams, SAP, Oracle, Workday, and major HRIS/CRM platforms. It's built to slot into complex enterprise tech stacks.

Sana integrates with major tools — Slack, GitHub, Salesforce, SharePoint, Notion, Jira — and its Enterprise tier adds HRIS connectors and open API access. The integration catalog is growing but not yet comparable to Docebo's breadth.

**However**: Sana's Workday acquisition changes the calculus. If you're a Workday shop, the integration of Sana's skill-mapping with Workday's talent data creates something no other vendor currently offers: learning pathways driven by actual HR and performance data.

### 6. User Experience and Ease of Use

**Winner: Sana Labs**

G2 ratings tell the story clearly:

| Metric | Docebo | Sana Labs |
|---|---|---|
| Ease of Use | 8.4 | 9.3 |
| Ease of Setup | 7.6 | 9.1 |
| Ease of Admin | 7.9 | 8.6 |

Sana's modern, clean interface reflects its 2016-era design sensibility. Docebo is powerful but carries the complexity of a platform that's been adding features for 20 years. Common Docebo criticisms mention admin complexity and unintuitive workflows for certain tasks; Sana criticisms focus more on missing features than usability problems.

### 7. Deployment Speed

**Winner: Sana Labs**

Sana deploys in 2–4 weeks. Docebo's Essential onboarding runs 6–12 weeks. For organizations that need to move fast, this is a meaningful difference.

### 8. Gamification and Engagement

**Winner: Docebo**

Docebo offers badges, leaderboards, point systems, and gamification mechanics as add-on modules. Sana's approach to engagement is more minimal — focused on adaptive learning paths and AI-driven personalization rather than traditional gamification elements.

If your training strategy relies on gamified engagement mechanics, Docebo delivers here. If you believe AI personalization is a better engagement driver than badges, Sana's approach may resonate more.

### 9. Mobile Learning

**Winner: Docebo**

Docebo offers native mobile apps, including a fully branded mobile app on the Enterprise tier with offline access and push notifications.

Sana Labs has **no native mobile app** as of 2026 — only mobile browser access. No offline mode, no push notifications. For desk-based knowledge workers, this may not matter. For frontline workers, healthcare staff, or field teams, it's a significant gap.

### 10. Analytics and Reporting

**Tie — different strengths**

Docebo's Advanced Analytics (Enterprise tier only) offers deep reporting, custom dashboards, and executive-ready visualizations built on years of enterprise reporting needs.

Sana's analytics use natural-language queries and predictive skill forecasting — genuinely innovative, letting admins ask questions in plain English rather than building reports. But the analytics capability is less mature for complex compliance and regulatory reporting.

## Who Should Choose What

### Choose Docebo If You:

- **Train multiple audiences** — employees, customers, partners, resellers — from one platform
- **Operate in regulated industries** where compliance automation, certifications, and audit trails are non-negotiable
- **Need a branded mobile app** with offline access for frontline or distributed teams
- **Have a complex tech stack** requiring 400+ integration options
- **Want e-commerce capabilities** to monetize training content
- **Need gamification** as a core engagement strategy
- **Prefer a publicly traded, independent vendor** with a 20-year track record

### Choose Sana Labs If You:

- **Prioritize AI-native learning** — not AI features grafted onto a traditional LMS, but AI as the foundation
- **Need fast deployment** — up to and running in 2–4 weeks vs. months
- **Focus primarily on internal employee training** rather than extended enterprise
- **Want consolidated tooling** — LMS + LXP + authoring + virtual classroom in one platform
- **Are a Workday customer** — the Sana integration with Workday talent data is a unique competitive advantage
- **Value ease of use** and want subject matter experts (not just instructional designers) building content
- **Need powerful knowledge retrieval** that searches across your entire internal knowledge base

### Think Twice If:

- **Sana + fewer than 300 users**: The minimum seat commitment makes Sana expensive for smaller organizations. Look at alternatives like TalentLMS or 360Learning.
- **Docebo + simple internal training only**: Docebo's power is in complexity and breadth. If you only need straightforward employee training, you're paying for capability you won't use.
- **Sana + frontline workers**: No native mobile app is a hard constraint for deskless workforces.
- **Docebo + tight budget with heavy AI usage**: The credit-based AI system can create unpredictable costs. Model your usage carefully before committing.

## For Experts, Academies, and Companies

**Experts** building and selling their knowledge through courses are unlikely to find either platform a natural fit — both are enterprise-oriented. Docebo's e-commerce module makes it *possible* for experts to monetize content at scale, but the price point and complexity are overkill for most independent experts. Sana has no e-commerce capability. Experts are better served by platforms built for that use case.

**Academies and training organizations** that deliver programs to external clients should lean toward **Docebo**. Its multi-tenant architecture, white-labeling, e-commerce, and extended enterprise capabilities are built for exactly this scenario. Sana's internal-training focus makes it a less natural fit for organizations whose business *is* training.

**Companies building internal training programs** face the real decision. If AI-native learning, fast deployment, and modern UX are top priorities, **Sana Labs** is compelling — especially for Workday shops. If you need multi-audience training, compliance infrastructure, gamification, mobile apps, and a battle-tested integration ecosystem, **Docebo** delivers on all fronts.

## The Verdict

Docebo and Sana Labs represent two different visions of where enterprise learning is heading.

**Docebo** is the established, feature-complete platform that can handle virtually any enterprise training scenario you throw at it. It's the choice when you need depth, compliance, multi-audience support, and a proven track record. The tradeoff is complexity, longer deployment, and a pricing model with a lot of moving parts.

**Sana Labs** is the AI-first bet on the future of learning. It's faster to deploy, easier to use, and its AI capabilities are genuinely deeper than what most traditional LMS platforms offer. The tradeoff is a younger platform with gaps (no mobile app, limited compliance tooling, thinner integration catalog) and the uncertainty of operating under Workday's ownership.

If you're choosing a platform that needs to work across complex enterprise scenarios today, Docebo is the proven choice. If you're choosing a platform that aligns with where AI-driven learning is heading and your use case is primarily internal training, Sana Labs is a strong and increasingly credible bet — particularly if Workday is already in your stack.

Both platforms justify their enterprise price tags. The question isn't which is "better" — it's which matches what you're actually trying to build.
