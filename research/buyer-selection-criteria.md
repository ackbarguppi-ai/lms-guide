LMS Buyer Selection Criteria Research
Purpose
This document captures the criteria buyers use when evaluating LMS, customer education, learning commerce, academy, membership, and community platforms.

It is intended to become a foundational website asset and to inform future:

comparison pages
buyer guides
vendor deep dives
positioning for Thinkific


Core hypothesis
Buyers are not choosing only on "LMS features." They are comparing platforms based on a broader set of jobs-to-be-done, including:

selling learning products
training customers
launching branded academies
building recurring membership/community businesses
supporting AI-enhanced learning experiences

That means the most useful comparison framework needs to cover more than lessons, quizzes, and reporting.


Main criteria buckets buyers appear to use
1) Use-case fit
Buyers first try to answer:

Is this for internal employee training?
Customer training?
Partner enablement?
Paid courses?
Cohort programs?
Memberships?
Communities?
Branded academies?

This is often the first shortlist filter.
2) Commerce and monetization
For many buyers, this is decisive.

Questions include:

Can I sell courses?
Can I sell programs?
Can I run subscriptions or memberships?
Can I bundle products together?
Can I support both B2C and B2B models?
How strong is checkout / payments / revenue support?

This area is especially important for platforms like Thinkific, where learning commerce is a meaningful differentiator.
3) Branding and ownership
Buyers care whether the learning experience feels like their business, not the vendor's.

Questions include:

Can I use my own domain?
Can I white-label the experience?
How customizable is the learner-facing site?
Can I create a branded academy?
Can I embed or extend the experience into my broader digital presence?
4) Learning product and learner experience
This is the most obvious bucket, but it is not the only one.

Questions include:

Course builder quality
Learning paths / programs
Assessments / quizzes / certificates
Community experience
Membership experience
Cohort or live-learning support
Ease of navigation for learners
5) Admin and operations
Buyers also compare how difficult the platform will be to run.

Questions include:

Ease of setup
Ease of ongoing management
User management
Permissions and roles
Reporting / analytics
Integrations
Multi-audience support
Reliability and support quality
6) AI capability
This category is becoming more important, but buyers likely compare it in layers.
Basic AI questions
Does it help create content?
Does it automate admin work?
Does it summarize or recommend?
Advanced AI questions
Can it support learners contextually?
Does it act like an assistant or agent?
Can it function like a teaching companion?
Is it just a gimmick or does it help outcomes?

This is where Thinker and similar agent-style capabilities become strategically important.
7) Scalability and organizational fit
Questions include:

Is this better for solo creators, SMBs, or enterprise?
Can it support multiple teams or audiences?
Can it handle external training at scale?
Can it grow with the business?
8) Price, cost, and implementation effort
Buyers compare not just sticker price, but total friction.

Questions include:

What does it cost?
What features sit behind higher plans?
How hard is implementation?
Will this require technical help?
What is the time-to-value?
9) Trust and proof
Buyers also look for evidence.

Sources often include:

review sites like G2
case studies
references
implementation stories
category and comparison articles

This area shapes credibility and final conversion.


Why this matters for the future website
The future site should not be structured as a shallow feature checklist.

It should be built around the criteria buyers actually use, such as:

use-case fit
monetization model
branding requirements
learner/community experience
AI capability
implementation effort
evidence from reviews and case studies

That will make the site:

more useful to buyers
more credible to LLMs and search engines
better at positioning Thinkific where it actually wins
