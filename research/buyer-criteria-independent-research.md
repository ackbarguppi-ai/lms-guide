## Research Methodology & Sources

This analysis draws from the following source categories:

- **Analyst reports**: Forrester Wave™ LMS & Experience Platforms (Q1 2024, Q4 2025); Gartner Peer Insights Corporate Learning Technologies (2026); ISG Buyers Guide for LMS (2025); TSIA State of Education Services (2024, 2025)
- **RFP analysis**: ListEdTech analysis of 69 LMS RFPs deconstructing evaluation criteria and weighting
- **Buyer guides & evaluation frameworks**: LMSpedia vendor evaluation checklist (2026); Learning Systems Authority evaluation framework; Skilljar/AlignedCX Customer Education LMS Evaluation Playbook
- **Review platform insights**: G2 "How to Choose an LMS" guide; Capterra buyer questions guide; LMS Portals synthesis of Reddit practitioner discussions
- **Industry-specific content**: Check-N-Click "10 Things to Know Before Buying a Customer Education Platform"; Blend-ed training academy buyer guide; DigitalChalk eCommerce LMS guide; LearnWorlds customer education LMS evaluation guide
- **Practitioner discussions**: Reddit threads aggregated by LMS Portals covering real-world feature priorities from instructional designers, IT admins, and L&D professionals

Key finding across all sources: **The #1 mistake buyers make is feature-checklist shopping instead of aligning the platform to their actual operational workflow and business model.** LMSpedia's analysis of 30+ implementations found that buying decisions fail most frequently "not because organizations choose 'bad' platforms, but because they choose platforms misaligned with operational reality."

## List A: Top 10 Buying Criteria for Training Academies

*Organizations whose primary business model is selling training — professional training companies, certification providers, online education businesses, subject matter experts who sell courses.*

### 1. E-Commerce & Revenue Engine Capabilities

**What it means in practice:** Built-in payment processing, flexible pricing models (one-time purchase, subscriptions, bundles, memberships, corporate licensing), discount/coupon management, tax handling, multi-currency support, and a branded public storefront/catalog that converts browsers into buyers.

**Why it ranks #1:** For training academies, the LMS IS the business. If it can't process payments, manage subscriptions, and present courses for sale effectively, nothing else matters. DigitalChalk's research notes that "traditional learning platforms were built for internal employee development, not to sell online courses to external audiences." The difference between a revenue-generating platform and a content-hosting platform starts here.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I sell individual courses, bundles, subscriptions, and corporate licenses from one platform?
- Does checkout happen inside my branded experience, or do learners get redirected to a third-party?
- Can I set up recurring billing with automated renewal?
- What are the transaction fees? (Some platforms take a percentage of every sale.)
- Can I handle multi-currency and international tax compliance?

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing a platform designed for internal corporate training and trying to bolt on e-commerce via plugins. This creates a fragmented buyer experience.
- Underestimating the importance of conversion-optimized storefronts. A clunky catalog with poor search/filter costs sales.
- Ignoring transaction fees that compound as revenue grows. A "free" platform that takes 10% of revenue becomes very expensive at scale.
- Starting on a marketplace (which limits branding, pricing control, and customer data ownership) and then facing painful migration later.

---

### 2. Course Delivery Flexibility (Self-Paced, ILT, Cohort, Blended)

**What it means in practice:** Support for multiple delivery modalities — self-paced on-demand courses, live instructor-led training (ILT) with scheduling and attendance tracking, cohort-based programs with defined timelines, and blended combinations of all three.

**Why it ranks #2:** Modern training companies don't just sell video courses. They run live workshops, cohort-based certification programs, blended bootcamps, and recurring scheduled classes. Blend-ed's 2026 buyer guide emphasizes that "selling courses in 2026 is no longer a content problem — it is an operations problem." A platform that only handles self-paced video is a dead end for professional training operations.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I schedule ILT sessions, assign instructors, and track attendance?
- Can I run cohort programs with enrollment windows, deadlines, and milestone tracking?
- Can I combine live sessions with on-demand modules in a single program?
- Does the platform support multiple instructors/trainers with their own dashboards?

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing a "creator-focused" platform (designed for solo course creators) when you need operational training delivery. These platforms focus on marketing funnels, not delivery workflows.
- Assuming you'll only ever need self-paced courses. Most successful training academies evolve toward blended delivery as they grow.
- Not testing the ILT workflow during evaluation. Many platforms claim ILT support but the actual admin experience is clunky.

---

### 3. Branding, White-Labeling & Learner Experience

**What it means in practice:** Full white-labeling — custom domains (academy.yourbrand.com), custom logos/colors/typography, branded certificates, branded emails, and a learner-facing experience that looks and feels like YOUR product, not a generic third-party tool.

**Why it ranks #3:** Your academy IS your brand. Check-N-Click's research states bluntly: "If your customer education portal looks like a generic third-party tool, you've already lost." For training academies selling premium courses, brand perception directly impacts willingness to pay and perceived value.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I use my own domain, not a subdomain of the vendor's site?
- Can I fully customize the visual design, or am I limited to choosing a template color?
- Are certificates, emails, and notifications all brandable?
- Does the learner-facing experience look professional and modern?
- Can I create different branded portals for different audiences or client organizations?

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting "you can add your logo" as white-labeling. True white-labeling means the entire experience — login, navigation, certificates, emails — carries your brand with no trace of the vendor.
- Not checking the mobile experience. Many platforms look great on desktop but deliver a poor mobile brand experience.
- Overlooking email branding. If course reminders and completion notifications come from "no-reply@vendor.com," it undermines your brand.

---

### 4. Scalability & Pricing That Doesn't Punish Growth

**What it means in practice:** The platform handles growth in learners, courses, and revenue without performance degradation or exponentially increasing costs. Pricing models that scale linearly (or better) rather than punishing success.

**Why it ranks #4:** Training academies that succeed will grow. A platform that works great at 50 learners but becomes slow or prohibitively expensive at 5,000 is a ticking time bomb. Check-N-Click recommends asking vendors directly: "What happens to my per-user cost at 10x scale? If they dodge the question, that's your answer."

**What buyers actually look for:**
- What's the pricing model? Per-user, per-active-user, flat-rate, revenue-share?
- What happens to unit costs at 10x my current learner count?
- Are there hard limits on storage, bandwidth, or concurrent users?
- Is multi-tenancy supported (serving different client organizations from one account)?
- Will load times remain acceptable as my catalog and user base grow?

**Common mistakes:**
- Not building a 3-year Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) model. LMSpedia found that "implementation effort, integrations, support tiers, internal labor, and scalability costs often exceed subscription fees over a 36-month horizon."
- Choosing based on Year 1 pricing without understanding how costs compound. A per-active-user model that's cheap at 200 users may be ruinous at 20,000.
- Ignoring hidden costs: implementation fees, premium support tiers, content migration charges, overage fees.

---

### 5. Content Creation & Management Tools

**What it means in practice:** Built-in course authoring tools (drag-and-drop editors, quiz builders, assessment creators), support for diverse content types (video, PDF, SCORM, xAPI, interactive modules), learning path creation, content versioning, and the ability to reuse content across multiple courses or programs.

**Why it ranks #5:** Training academies are constantly creating and updating content. Reddit practitioners consistently highlight that "instructional designers save significant time when an LMS allows reusable templates" and built-in authoring eliminates the need for expensive third-party tools. AI-assisted course creation is emerging as a genuine time-saver (not just marketing hype) when it reduces operational effort.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I build courses directly in the platform, or do I need separate authoring software?
- Does it support SCORM, xAPI, and cmi5 for imported content?
- Can I create learning paths with prerequisites and sequential unlocking?
- Does it handle video well (hosting, streaming, chaptering)?
- Is there version control so I can update content without disrupting active learners?
- Are AI authoring features genuinely useful or just marketing checkboxes?

**Common mistakes:**
- Buying a platform with limited authoring and then spending $10K+ annually on separate authoring tools.
- Ignoring content standards (SCORM/xAPI). If you ever need to migrate to a different platform, proprietary content formats trap you.
- Over-investing in AI features without testing them. Per Check-N-Click: "Ask for a demo of the AI features. If they can't show you tangible value in 10 minutes, it's probably not there."

---

### 6. Certification & Credentialing Management

**What it means in practice:** Automated certificate generation and issuance, certificate verification (shareable links, blockchain verification), renewal/expiration management, continuing education credit tracking, and recertification workflows.

**Why it ranks #6:** For many training academies, certifications ARE the product. Professional certifications, compliance credentials, and continuing education programs represent recurring revenue through renewal cycles. DigitalChalk notes these are "primary revenue drivers" for organizations selling training.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can certificates be auto-generated with custom branding upon course/program completion?
- Is there built-in renewal/expiration tracking with automated reminders?
- Can certificates be verified externally (verification URLs, LinkedIn sharing)?
- Does it support CE/CPE credit tracking for regulated industries?
- Can I build structured recertification pathways?

**Common mistakes:**
- Treating certification as a nice-to-have rather than a core revenue feature. Certifications create recurring revenue through renewal cycles.
- Not planning for recertification workflows. Many platforms handle initial certification but lack renewal automation.
- Ignoring the verification question. Employers and regulators increasingly want to verify credentials independently.

---

### 7. Analytics & Reporting Beyond Completion Rates

**What it means in practice:** Dashboards showing learner progress, engagement metrics (time-on-task, drop-off points, assessment performance), revenue reporting, cohort analysis, and the ability to export data for external reporting or regulatory audits.

**Why it ranks #7:** Check-N-Click makes the case clearly: "Completion rates are vanity metrics. Someone clicking 'Next' through 12 slides doesn't mean they learned anything." For training academies, analytics serve two purposes — improving the learning product and demonstrating value to B2B clients.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I see WHERE learners struggle, not just IF they completed?
- Do I get revenue analytics alongside learning analytics?
- Can I generate client-level reports for B2B training contracts?
- Are reports exportable and schedulable?
- Can I correlate training completion with real-world outcomes?

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming all LMS reporting is the same. Many platforms only track completion, not engagement depth.
- Not asking about revenue reporting. For training academies, sales data is just as important as learning data.
- Ignoring API access for reporting. If you need to connect analytics to a BI tool, REST API access is essential.

---

### 8. Marketing & Sales Integration

**What it means in practice:** Landing pages, SEO-friendly course pages, email marketing integration, affiliate/referral programs, lead capture, and integration with marketing automation tools.

**Why it ranks #8:** Training academies need to attract and convert buyers. Unlike internal corporate LMS deployments where learners are assigned, training academies must market and sell. Integration with email platforms, CRM systems, and marketing automation tools is critical for growth.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Does the platform generate SEO-friendly, indexable course pages?
- Can I build landing pages and sales pages within the platform?
- Does it integrate with my email marketing tool and CRM?
- Can I run affiliate or referral programs?
- Does it support discount codes, promotions, and urgency-based marketing?

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing an "enterprise LMS" designed for internal training that has zero marketing capability. These platforms assume learners are assigned, not acquired.
- Over-weighting marketing features versus delivery features. Some platforms are marketing-first but weak on the actual learning experience.
- Not thinking about SEO. If your course pages can't be indexed by search engines, you're invisible to organic traffic.

---

### 9. B2B/Multi-Client Support

**What it means in practice:** The ability to serve multiple corporate clients from one platform — with separate portals, branding, user management, and reporting for each client organization. Sometimes called "multi-tenancy" or "multi-portal."

**Why it ranks #9:** Successful training academies eventually sell to organizations, not just individuals. B2B clients expect their own branded portal, dedicated reporting, and centralized user management. Without multi-client architecture, serving B2B clients requires painful workarounds.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I create separate, branded portals for each corporate client?
- Can each client manage their own users while I manage the overall platform?
- Can I generate client-specific reports?
- Can I set up corporate billing (invoicing, POs) alongside individual purchases?
- Is there a limit on the number of client portals?

**Common mistakes:**
- Not planning for B2B even if you're starting B2C. Most training academies that survive eventually need B2B capability.
- Choosing a platform that charges per-portal fees that become prohibitive as client count grows.
- Underestimating the importance of client-level reporting. B2B clients will demand it.

---

### 10. Support, Onboarding & Vendor Partnership

**What it means in practice:** Quality of vendor support (response times, channels, SLAs), onboarding assistance, migration help, documentation depth, and whether the vendor acts as a strategic partner or just a software provider.

**Why it ranks #10:** The Skilljar/AlignedCX playbook emphasizes that "most buyers skip this, and it's arguably the most important" — but in practice, buyers consistently rank it lower during evaluation and only feel its importance post-purchase. LMSpedia found that implementation complexity is "the most common pitfall" — vendors quote 4-6 weeks for setup but ignore the 3-4 months of internal work.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- What are the support SLAs? 24/7 or business hours only?
- Is there a dedicated customer success manager, or just a ticket queue?
- What does onboarding look like? Vendor-led or self-service?
- Is content migration included or an additional cost?
- How robust is the documentation and knowledge base?
- Does the vendor have a community or user group?

**Common mistakes:**
- Not checking support quality during evaluation. Ask for references and ask specifically about post-go-live support.
- Assuming "basic support" is adequate. Many platforms' basic tier means 48-hour response times.
- Underestimating migration effort. Content, user data, and certifications rarely port cleanly between platforms.

## List B: Top 10 Buying Criteria for Companies Adding Customer Training

*Companies with 25+ employees whose primary business is NOT training, but are adding customer-facing training (paid or free). SaaS companies, manufacturers, service companies, financial services firms educating their customers.*

### 1. Integration with Existing Business Systems (CRM, CS, Product)

**What it means in practice:** Deep, bidirectional integration with your CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot), customer success platform (Gainsight), product analytics, support/helpdesk tools, and identity management (SSO via SAML/OAuth). Not just "it has an API" but native integrations that pass meaningful data between systems.

**Why it ranks #1:** For companies adding customer training, the LMS is NOT the core business — it must serve the core business. The Skilljar/AlignedCX playbook found that "integrations aren't just technical — they're how you make education matter to the business." If the LMS can't tell your CS team which customers completed onboarding training, or your CRM which accounts are certified, the platform is a silo. Check-N-Click states it plainly: "If your customer education platform can't sync with Salesforce, HubSpot, or your customer success tool, you're flying blind."

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Does it natively integrate with my CRM? Can I see training completion data in customer records?
- Can I trigger workflows based on learning milestones (e.g., auto-update health score when onboarding is complete)?
- Does it support SSO so customers don't need another login?
- Can I sync user data with my identity provider?
- Is data sync real-time or batch (weekly uploads)?

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting "we have an API" as sufficient. APIs require engineering resources to build and maintain custom integrations. Native, pre-built connectors save months of work.
- Not involving IT early in evaluation. The Skilljar playbook found that "IT selects technically sound platform but L&D finds content authoring unusable — or L&D chooses user-friendly platform IT can't integrate."
- Overlooking SSO. Every additional login is a barrier to customer engagement. Customers who must create a separate account for your academy often won't bother.

---

### 2. Speed to Launch & Ease of Administration

**What it means in practice:** How quickly a non-technical team can get the platform live with courses available to customers. Simple admin interface, intuitive course creation, minimal IT dependency for day-to-day operations.

**Why it ranks #2:** These companies are NOT in the training business. The team managing customer education is usually small (often 1-3 people initially), wears multiple hats, and cannot spend 6 months on implementation. Reddit practitioners consistently highlight wanting "fast setup, intuitive workflows, and minimal training needed to get started." LMSpedia notes that "simple cloud deployments (minimal legacy data, <500 users) should take 6-10 weeks."

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can a non-technical person create and publish a course in under a day?
- How long from contract signing to first customer taking a course?
- Does everyday administration require IT involvement?
- Is the admin interface intuitive or does it require extensive training?
- Can I manage the platform with 1-2 people, not a dedicated team?

**Common mistakes:**
- Buying an enterprise LMS designed for organizations with dedicated L&D teams of 10+. The admin complexity will overwhelm a small customer education team.
- Underestimating implementation time. LMSpedia warns that vendors "quote 4-6 weeks because they only account for technical setup" and ignore content creation, data cleanup, and stakeholder training.
- Choosing based on the demo without testing admin workflows. Demos showcase best-case scenarios. Request sandbox access and have your actual team test it.

---

### 3. Learner Experience & Customer-Facing UX

**What it means in practice:** The learning experience your customers encounter — clean modern design, intuitive navigation, clear progress tracking, fast load times, mobile responsiveness, and accessibility compliance (WCAG 2.1 AA).

**Why it ranks #3:** Your customers are comparing the learning experience to every other digital product they use. Check-N-Click warns: "If your academy feels like navigating a 2005 intranet, they'll bounce and never come back (hello, endless support tickets)." A poor learner experience doesn't just reduce training completion — it reflects badly on your entire brand and product.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Is the learner interface clean and modern, or does it look like enterprise software?
- Can learners easily find, start, and resume courses?
- Is progress tracking visible and motivating?
- Load times under 3 seconds?
- Does it work well on mobile without a dedicated app?
- Is it accessible (WCAG 2.1 AA)?

**Common mistakes:**
- Only evaluating the admin interface during demos. The Skilljar playbook recommends: "Request a learner-view demo, not just an admin walkthrough. Better yet, have someone outside your team test it cold."
- Assuming mobile-responsive equals mobile-ready. A responsive layout is bare minimum. True mobile experience includes optimized touch interactions, offline access, and push notifications.
- Ignoring accessibility. Beyond legal exposure (ADA Title III has been applied to digital platforms), poor accessibility excludes customers who need your training most.

---

### 4. Branding & White-Labeling

**What it means in practice:** Custom domains, full visual customization, branded certificates, branded emails — the academy looks like an extension of your product, not a third-party tool.

**Why it ranks #4:** For companies adding customer training, brand coherence matters enormously. The academy is an extension of your product. If it looks different from your website and product, customers lose trust. LearnWorlds' evaluation guide emphasizes: "Choose a white-label learning management system, so your brand gets the spotlight."

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Custom domain (learn.ourcompany.com)
- Full control over colors, fonts, layout, and navigation
- Branded certificates and credentials
- Consistent branding across all customer touchpoints (emails, notifications, login screens)
- Per-audience branding if serving multiple customer segments

**Common mistakes:**
- Same as Training Academies — accepting logo-swap as "white-labeling."
- Not checking email and notification branding. Automated emails from the LMS vendor's domain undermine your brand.

---

### 5. Analytics Tied to Business Outcomes (Not Just Completions)

**What it means in practice:** The ability to measure how training impacts business metrics — product adoption, support ticket volume, customer retention, NPS, expansion revenue. Not just "who completed what" but "did training change customer behavior?"

**Why it ranks #5:** This is WHERE this buyer profile diverges most sharply from training academies. TSIA's State of Education Services 2025 research found that "product adoption" has been the #1 stated objective for education services teams since 2020 — but most can't prove training drives adoption because their analytics don't connect learning data to product data. The Skilljar playbook states: "Reporting shouldn't just count course completions. It should connect learning activity to adoption, retention, expansion, and advocacy."

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I correlate training completion with product adoption metrics?
- Can I see which trained customers have lower churn rates?
- Does the platform integrate with my BI tools for custom analysis?
- Can I demonstrate ROI to executives in their language (retention, NRR, support deflection)?
- Can I identify at-risk customers based on training engagement?

**Common mistakes:**
- Settling for completion-rate reporting and calling it "analytics." TSIA found that 24-26% of education services leaders couldn't even report basic training consumption rates.
- Not planning the data pipeline. Even with great LMS analytics, connecting learning data to business outcomes requires integration with CRM and product analytics.
- Using vanity metrics to justify the program internally. Executives care about retention, adoption, and revenue — not badges earned.

---

### 6. Scalability & Multi-Audience Support

**What it means in practice:** The ability to serve different customer segments (enterprise vs. SMB, different products, different regions) with tailored learning experiences, while scaling from hundreds to tens of thousands of learners without performance issues or cost explosions.

**Why it ranks #6:** Companies adding customer training often need to serve diverse audiences — new users need onboarding, power users need advanced training, partners need certification, different products need different academies. The Skilljar playbook identifies multi-tenant/multi-audience capability as a key "strategic enabler" that differentiates platforms. LearnWorlds highlights localization and multilanguage support as critical scalability factors.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I create different learning experiences for different customer segments?
- Does it support multiple languages and regions?
- Is the pricing model sustainable at 10x my current user count?
- Can I add partner training alongside customer training?
- Does performance hold up under load?

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting with a simple tool that can't handle audience segmentation, then needing to migrate as the program grows.
- Not planning for global audiences even if starting domestic. Localization is extremely painful to add later.
- Ignoring partner/channel training needs. Many companies discover they need partner enablement shortly after launching customer training.

---

### 7. Content Management & Rapid Updates

**What it means in practice:** The ability to quickly create, update, and organize training content — especially important when your product ships frequent updates that require training materials to stay current.

**Why it ranks #7:** Unlike training academies that may update content quarterly, companies training customers on their product need to update training materials every time the product ships a new release. LearnWorlds emphasizes the need for "version control to keep track of who's done what and when." AI-assisted content creation is especially valuable here for keeping pace with product updates.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can non-technical team members update course content quickly?
- Is there version control?
- Can I organize content into logical structures (learning paths, collections)?
- Does it support various content formats (video, docs, interactive, SCORM)?
- Can AI help me keep content current as my product changes?

**Common mistakes:**
- Not planning for the content maintenance burden. Creating courses is the easy part; keeping them current as the product evolves is the ongoing cost.
- Building everything as video. Video is expensive and slow to update. A mix of formats (including text and interactive) allows faster updates.
- Not connecting content to the product team's release cycle. If content updates lag product releases, customers get trained on outdated workflows.

---

### 8. E-Commerce & Monetization (When Applicable)

**What it means in practice:** The ability to charge for premium training, certifications, or advanced courses — even if much of your customer education is free. Payment processing, subscription management, and revenue reporting.

**Why it ranks #8 (not higher):** Unlike training academies where revenue IS the business model, many companies adding customer training start with free training to drive adoption and retention. Monetization often comes later for advanced/certification content. The Skilljar playbook positions commerce as a "strategic enabler" and recommends: "Validate that your LMS can handle ecommerce at scale and supports the partner distribution models relevant to your GTM strategy." TSIA research confirms that leading organizations are adopting "education-led growth" frameworks that include direct revenue from training.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Can I offer some training free and some paid from the same platform?
- Does it support subscriptions, bundles, and corporate licensing?
- Can I handle invoicing for enterprise clients (not just credit card checkout)?
- Is revenue reporting built in?
- Can I start free and add monetization later without re-platforming?

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing a platform with zero e-commerce capability and then being stuck when leadership asks "why aren't we monetizing this?"
- Over-engineering monetization before proving the training program works. Start with adoption impact, then monetize.
- Ignoring corporate/B2B billing. Enterprise clients expect invoicing and PO support, not just Stripe checkout.

---

### 9. Certification & Credentialing

**What it means in practice:** Automated certificate issuance, branded credentials, verification mechanisms, and (for some) continuing education credit management. Increasingly includes digital badges shareable on LinkedIn.

**Why it ranks #9:** Certifications serve multiple purposes for this buyer profile: they validate customer competency (important for complex products), create customer loyalty/stickiness, and can be monetized. The Skilljar playbook includes "robust cert lifecycle (issuance, expiry, re-cert), proctoring integrations, CE/CPE credit tracking, LinkedIn sharing" as a strategic enabler.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Auto-generated, branded certificates upon completion
- Verification URLs for employers/partners
- LinkedIn sharing capability
- Renewal/expiration workflows for recurring certifications
- Assessment-based certification (not just "watched the videos")

**Common mistakes:**
- Treating certificates as decoration rather than strategic tools. A well-designed certification program drives adoption, creates stickiness, and can become a revenue stream.
- Issuing certificates without meaningful assessments. A certificate for watching 3 videos has no credibility.

---

### 10. Total Cost of Ownership & Vendor Stability

**What it means in practice:** The full cost picture over 3+ years — including subscription fees, implementation, integration development, content migration, ongoing support, and internal labor. Also: the vendor's financial stability, product roadmap, and likelihood of being around in 5 years.

**Why it ranks #10:** LMSpedia's analysis is definitive: "Total Cost of Ownership matters more than list pricing. Implementation effort, integrations, support tiers, internal labor, and scalability costs often exceed subscription fees over a 36-month horizon." For companies where the LMS isn't the core product, getting stuck with an unstable vendor or surprise costs creates organizational pain that distracts from the actual business.

**What buyers actually look for:**
- Full TCO breakdown for Years 1, 2, and 3
- Implementation costs (vendor-led vs. self-service)
- Integration development costs
- What's included in the base price vs. what requires premium tiers
- Vendor's financial stability, customer count, and years in operation
- Roadmap transparency — does the vendor's direction align with my needs?
- Data portability — how hard is it to leave?

**Common mistakes:**
- Evaluating on list price alone. A "$500/month" platform with $15K implementation, $5K/year premium support, and $3K/year integration maintenance is actually $28K in Year 1.
- Not asking about data portability. If you need to switch vendors, can you export your content, user data, and completion records in usable formats?
- Ignoring vendor stability signals. Small vendors may offer great features but lack the financial stability for long-term partnership.

## List C: Third Buyer Segment — Partner/Channel Enablement

*A meaningful third segment emerged from the research: companies training partners, resellers, distributors, or channel networks on their products. This segment has distinct criteria from both training academies and customer education programs.*

This segment shares characteristics with both buyer profiles but has unique requirements:

**Key differentiating criteria:**
1. **Multi-tenant architecture** is non-negotiable (each partner needs their own branded environment)
2. **Role-based access and delegation** — partners need admin control over their own users
3. **Co-branding capability** — the platform must show the vendor's content in the partner's brand
4. **Compliance and certification tracking** at the partner-organization level
5. **Reporting by partner organization** — which partners are trained, which aren't, how does training correlate with partner performance?

The Skilljar playbook specifically calls out partner training as a distinct use case requiring "multi-tenant/multi-audience" capability as a strategic enabler. The Forrester Wave evaluates platforms partly on their ability to serve "extended enterprise" audiences including partners and channels.

This segment is not detailed into a full top-10 here because the core criteria heavily overlap with Customer Training (List B), with multi-tenancy and delegated administration being the primary additions.

## Overlap and Divergence Analysis

### Where the Lists Overlap (Shared Priorities)

| Criterion | Training Academy Rank | Customer Training Rank | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Branding & White-Labeling | #3 | #4 | Both need branded experiences, but for different reasons — academies for premium perception, companies for brand coherence |
| Scalability | #4 | #6 | Both need to grow, but academies worry about pricing models while companies worry about audience segmentation |
| Content Management | #5 | #7 | Both need to create/manage content, but companies need faster update cycles tied to product releases |
| Certification | #6 | #9 | Academies treat certs as a core product; companies treat them as a strategic tool |
| Analytics | #7 | #5 | Both need analytics but measure fundamentally different things (revenue vs. adoption) |
| E-Commerce | #1 | #8 | The widest gap — e-commerce is existential for academies but optional/secondary for companies |

### Where the Lists Diverge (Different Priorities)

**Training Academies prioritize but Companies don't (as much):**
- **E-Commerce (#1 vs. #8):** The most dramatic divergence. For academies, revenue capability IS the selection criteria. For companies, it's nice-to-have.
- **Course Delivery Flexibility (#2):** Academies need ILT, cohorts, and blended delivery. Companies typically start with self-paced only.
- **Marketing & Sales Integration (#8):** Academies need to attract buyers. Companies already have customers — they need to activate them.
- **B2B/Multi-Client Support (#9):** Academies sell to organizations. Companies serve their own customers.

**Companies Adding Training prioritize but Academies don't (as much):**
- **Integration with Business Systems (#1):** The single biggest differentiator. Companies MUST connect the LMS to their CRM, CS platform, and product. Academies operate more independently.
- **Speed to Launch (#2):** Companies need to go live fast with a small team. Academies are more willing to invest in lengthy setup because the LMS IS their core infrastructure.
- **Learner UX (#3):** Companies worry more because their customers are comparing the experience to the core product. Academies' learners are more motivated (they paid for the training).
- **Analytics Tied to Business Outcomes (#5):** Companies need to prove training drives adoption and retention. Academies need to prove it generates revenue. Different metrics entirely.

### WHY the Priorities Differ

The fundamental difference comes down to one question: **Is training your product, or does training serve your product?**

- **Training Academies:** Training IS the product. The LMS is the primary revenue-generating infrastructure. They optimize for sales, delivery sophistication, and brand premium.
- **Companies Adding Training:** Training SERVES the product. The LMS must integrate into the existing business stack and prove it impacts customer success metrics. They optimize for integration, speed, and measurable business outcomes.

This distinction explains every divergence:
- Academies need e-commerce because that's how they make money. Companies may not charge for training at all.
- Academies need ILT/cohort support because they sell premium learning experiences. Companies need self-paced content that scales without human facilitation.
- Academies need marketing features because they must acquire learners. Companies already have learners (their customers) — they need to activate them.
- Companies need CRM/CS integration because training must serve the broader customer success motion. Academies operate the LMS as a standalone business.
