Expanded Competitor Universe
Purpose
This document expands the LMS research set beyond the most obvious hosted and enterprise names.

The future site should not assume buyers compare only a few mainstream LMS vendors. In reality, they often compare across:

hosted learning commerce platforms
enterprise LMSs
open-source systems
WordPress/Joomla-based stacks
custom LMS providers
AI-native learning platforms

This document captures additional competitor categories and examples that should be part of the broader market map.


1) Open-source and Moodle-derived territory
Open LMS
Source: https://www.openlms.net/

Key positioning signals

“Fully customizable open-source LMS that fits your needs”
powered by AWS and Moodle
serves business and education segments
offers AI-powered tools, analytics, compliance, customization, ecommerce, hosting/support, and professional services
explicit verticals include corporate, extended enterprise, government, healthcare, higher education, professional associations, and training companies

Interpretation Open LMS matters because it represents a more managed/open-source-derived path for buyers who want:

customization
Moodle lineage
enterprise or extended-enterprise support
more control than a typical SaaS platform

It belongs in the research set as an important comparator for buyers weighing:

hosted SaaS simplicity versus
open/customizable infrastructure


2) AI-native enterprise learning territory
Sana / Sana Labs
Source: https://sanalabs.com/

Key positioning signals

“Superintelligence for work”
Sana Learn positioned as “the best of an LMS, LXP, authoring tool, and virtual classroom in one AI-native learning platform”
emphasizes:
create content faster
tutor at everyone’s fingertips
admin on autopilot
granular insights quickly
broader Sana platform positions around enterprise AI for knowledge, workflows, and action

Interpretation Sana matters because it is not simply “an LMS with AI features.” It is closer to an AI-native learning/work platform.

This makes Sana important for comparison wherever buyers care deeply about:

AI tutoring/assistance
AI-native learning workflows
enterprise upskilling / knowledge enablement
modern learning stack consolidation

This is a particularly important reference point when evaluating how differentiated Thinkific’s AI story really is.


3) Custom LMS / bespoke platform territory
Plume / Plume Studio
Sources

https://plumestudio.com/
https://plumestudio.com/services/custom-learning-platforms
https://elearningindustry.com/directory/elearning-software/plume-lms

Key positioning signals surfaced

custom LMS development and training infrastructure studio
bespoke LMS design and development
positioned around ownership, UX, custom business models, ecommerce, reporting, automation, and replacing patchwork systems

Interpretation Plume matters because some buyers are not choosing between SaaS LMS vendors at all. They are choosing between:

buying a platform
or building/commissioning a custom one

This is especially relevant for:

larger training businesses
B2C education brands
organizations with unusual workflows or learner experiences
businesses that see the platform itself as strategic IP

The future site should likely address this fork directly:

hosted LMS vs custom LMS


4) WordPress / self-assembled stack territory
LearnDash
Source: https://www.learndash.com/

Research note Lightweight scraping returned mostly consent/cookie material, so richer capture is still needed.

Why it matters anyway LearnDash is strategically important because it represents a common buyer path:

WordPress-first
plugin-based stack
more ownership/control
more responsibility for assembly and maintenance

LearnDash should remain in the competitor set because many buyers compare:

all-in-one hosted platform versus
WordPress-based flexibility

That is a meaningful buying decision, even when feature overlap is partial.


5) Joomla-based LMS territory
Joomla LMS options surfaced
Reference links

JoomLMS: https://www.joomlms.com/
Guru / iJoomla LMS: https://guru.ijoomla.com/
SP LMS: https://www.joomshaper.com/joomla-extensions/sp-lms
OSCampus: https://www.joomlashack.com/joomla-extensions/oscampus/
Shika LMS: https://techjoomla.com/shika

Interpretation Joomla LMS tools are probably not first-tier strategic competitors for Thinkific in most modern buying cycles, but they matter as evidence of a broader pattern:

some buyers still want CMS-native control
some want on-premise/self-managed deployment
some compare “learning platform” against existing CMS ecosystems rather than against dedicated SaaS learning businesses

This territory is useful more as a category contrast than as the main frontline competitor set.


6) What this means for the future site
The future site should not act as if the market contains only:

Thinkific
Docebo
Absorb
LearnWorlds

The real market includes additional decision routes such as:

open-source / managed open-source (Moodle, Open LMS)
AI-native learning/work platforms (Sana)
custom LMS development (Plume)
WordPress/Joomla self-assembled stacks (LearnDash, Joomla LMS options)

That matters because buyers are often asking bigger questions such as:

Do I want a hosted SaaS platform or more control?
Do I need learning commerce or enterprise learning operations?
Do I need a custom platform?
Is AI a real differentiator here?
Am I solving for speed, ownership, flexibility, or scale?

Bottom line
The competitor universe is wider than standard LMS comparison lists suggest.

To build a genuinely useful site, we should include not only:

hosted learning commerce platforms
enterprise LMSs

but also:

open-source / managed-open-source systems
AI-native learning platforms
custom LMS providers
WordPress/Joomla-based alternatives

That broader map will make the eventual site more realistic, more useful, and better at helping buyers reach the right conclusion.
